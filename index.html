KEY ACTION #1

1

Activate an Emergency Operations Center and establish a whole-of-community incident management structure

Controlling a pandemic outbreak is a multi-disciplinary and whole-of-society endeavor; it requires engagement and coordination among government leaders, public health officials, civil society and religious leaders, the business community, academia, and others. The official response, and public reaction, is likely to have other community impacts such as supply chain disruption and public safety workforce shortages.

Additionally, the EOC must begin to plan for potential attrition of first responders by establishing a continuity of operations plan to replace and supplement critical personnel. A best practice from previous novel outbreak responses is to establish an EOC to host a whole-of-community incident management structure, leveraging existing plans and organizations where possible, that brings these domains together to streamline communication, planning, decision-making, and operational coordination. Ensure EOC iseffectively communicating and aligned with higher-level EOC processes and decision cycles through liaison exchange whenever possible.

    Phase 0

  1. Have you activated your emergency plans and emergency operations centers to assist with coordination of:
    1. Hospitals, public health, EMS and other key elements of the health and medical sector
    2. Civil society groups, religious institutions, and other community leaders
    3. Appropriate representation from all departments and agencies
  2. Have you designated or appointed a response coordinator with:
    1. The authority to convene community and business leaders and officials across all departments and agencies?
    2. The authority to bring issues directly to you?
    3. The ability to connect with other state, local, and federal officials in charge of the response?
    4. Links to technical advisory experts to keep abreast of emerging research
  3. Do you have a Continuity of Government plan to ensure continued essential services?
  4. Is there a clearly defined process by which health and medical stakeholders advance resource requests to the jurisdictional EOC?
  5. What declarations or legal/regulatory guidance has been implemented and how do they impact the decisions that need to be made?
  6. Is there a process in place to ensure that timely, accurate risk communications are available and coordinated with all jurisdictional agencies? Are public health information specialists integrated into the Joint Information System?
  7. Is your EOC organized with teams with responsibilities for each key action?

    8. Phase 1

  8. Have you activated your emergency plans and emergency operations centers to assist with coordination?

KEY ACTION #2

2

Understand the real-time disease spread of COVID-19 in your community

The elements of a pandemic control strategy begin with understanding transmission and clinical severity dynamics in the community. This entails amplifying surveillance, diagnostic testing, and reporting systems to ensure that data on outbreak dynamics is available on a real-time basis to inform strategy-setting and daily tactical decision-making. All of this is currently difficult due to testing shortfalls; scaling up local access to testing will be critical as more laboratory capacity comes on line over the coming weeks.

KEY ACTION #3

3

Delay or reduce transmission, #FlattenTheCurve

Slowing and limiting transmission through a community is a critical way of reducing the human cost of the outbreak, and ensuring that hospitals will be able to deliver lifesaving and life sustaining care. It can also be highly disruptive, as social distancing measures must become more aggressive in proportion to the exponential spread of the virus. Determinations on the best means of limiting transmission will depend on local factors, but in general should be calibrated to be more aggressive than what observable local conditions might intuitively suggest.

KEY ACTION #4

4

Focus protection on high risk groups and facilities

COVID-19 poses extreme risks to older populations and those with complicating health conditions. Each local context is unique and each local EOC should create a commonly agreed and regularly reassessed list of particularly vulnerable demographics. Particular attention must be paid to the needs of these populations, and the facilities where they may reside or gather. Reducing transmission among these groups through targeted support measures can help protect them while also alleviating pressure on healthcare systems.

KEY ACTION #5

5

Reinforce and expand health system surge capacity to avoid high mortality

The mortality risk posed by COVID-19 can grow substantially if a health system becomes overwhelmed with critical cases. Urgent action should be taken to minimize the risk of transmission within health settings (nosocomial transmission) and to find creative means of stretching or expanding existing critical care capacity. Telehealth consultations, including the use of telemedicine oversight of critically ill patients in the emergency department and intensive care units, should be considered as an option to triage cases as well as manage patient care, permitting the continued engagement of clinicians who may have been isolated due to infection, but can still provide medical oversight.

KEY ACTION #6

6

Risk communication and community engagement

Public officials have a solemn obligation to accurately and transparently relay risk information, even (or perhaps especially) when it is alarming. Community trust can make or break an outbreak response, because the effectiveness of social distancing and other interventions hinges on community compliance. Risk communication should follow best practices and to mobilize informed action rather than inducing panic. A set of CIRs (Critical Information Requirements for leaders/decision makers) should be set by EOC, constantly updated daily, and should help feed a set of PIRs (Public Information Requirements that should be communicated to affected populations) that are easily accessible online and constantly updated.

KEY ACTION #7

7

Mitigating economic and social consequences

Pandemic outbreaks can cause enormous social and economic disruption. This is damaging in its own right, but can be particularly problematic if it creates economic disincentives to cooperating with social distancing measures. Mitigating these disruptions can help to reduce the human cost of the outbreak, beyond the immediate toll of the disease itself. Leaders should also pay careful attention to the impact that both the outbreak itself, and the measures to control it, may have on vulnerable populations.
