Flatten the Curve: a Frontline Guide for Local Decision-Makers

The COVID-19 pandemic is causing significant disruptions to cities and local communities globally. Given these challenges, we have developed this guide to provide a decision framework for local leaders to think through what will need to be done to help reduce the impact of the outbreak, both by reducing spread and decreasing the number of cases, but also in responding and supporting communities effectively. Key actions for local decision makers

KEY ACTION #1

1

Activate an Emergency Operations Center and establish a whole-of-community incident management structure

Controlling a pandemic outbreak is a multi-disciplinary and whole-of-society endeavor; it requires engagement and coordination among government leaders, public health officials, civil society and religious leaders, the business community, academia, and others. The official response, and public reaction, is likely to have other community impacts such as supply chain disruption and public safety workforce shortages.

Additionally, the EOC must begin to plan for potential attrition of first responders by establishing a continuity of operations plan to replace and supplement critical personnel. A best practice from previous novel outbreak responses is to establish an EOC to host a whole-of-community incident management structure, leveraging existing plans and organizations where possible, that brings these domains together to streamline communication, planning, decision-making, and operational coordination. Ensure EOC iseffectively communicating and aligned with higher-level EOC processes and decision cycles through liaison exchange whenever possible.

    PHASE

    No detected cases
    No burden on healthcare

  1. Have you activated your emergency plans and emergency operations centers to assist with coordination of:
    1. Healthcare coalitions, including hospitals, public health, EMS and other key elements of the health and medical sector?
    2. Civil society groups, religious institutions, and other community leaders?
    3. Appropriate representation from all departments and agencies?
  2. Have you designated or appointed a response coordinator with:
    1. The authority to convene community and business leaders and officials across all departments and agencies?
    2. The authority to bring issues directly to you?
    3. The ability to connect with other state, local, and federal officials in charge of the response?
    4. Linkages to technical advisory experts to keep abreast of emerging research?
  3. Do you have a Continuity of Government plan to ensure continued essential services?
  4. Is there a clearly delineated process by which health and medical stakeholders advance resource requests to the jurisdictional EOC?
  5. What key communications systems and technologies are needed in your EOC?
  6. How will you begin to build and maintain a common operating picture to share situational awareness with all key partners?
  7. What declarations or legal/regulatory guidance has been implemented and how do they impact the decisions that need to be made?
  8. Is there a process in place to ensure that timely, accurate risk communications are available and coordinated with all jurisdictional agencies? Are public health information specialists integrated into the Joint Information System?
  9. Is your EOC organized with teams with responsibilities for each key action?

    10. PHASE

    Individually detected cases,
    traceable to know sources of transmission

  10. Have you activated your emergency plans and EOCs to assist with coordination?
  11. Have you defined the goals for the response and values you want to preserve in the response?
  12. What special health measures have you implemented in your EOC to ensure the health and wellbeing of personnel assigned to work there?
  13. As quarantines and self-isolation requirements expand, is there a way to transition to a virtual EOC for non-essential personnel who still need to be engaged as well as those who may be infected?
  14. Have you developed a process to maintain a common operating picture during a prolonged event?

KEY ACTION #2

2

Understand the real-time spread of disease in your community

The elements of a pandemic control strategy begin with understanding transmission and clinical severity dynamics in the community. This entails amplifying surveillance, diagnostic testing, and reporting systems to ensure that data on outbreak dynamics is available on a real-time basis to inform strategy-setting and daily tactical decision-making. All of this is currently difficult due to testing shortfalls; scaling up local access to testing will be critical as more laboratory capacity comes on line over the coming weeks.

    PHASE

    No detected cases
    No burden on healthcare

  1. Does your response system allow your EOC to receive notice of all cases tested in your community from both public and private health facilities?
  2. Are hospitals and clinics in your area using syndromic surveillance to detect abnormal levels of respiratory or flu-like cases, inconsistent with normal seasonal progression? Are these testing negative for influenza or other common viruses?
  3. Is your community able to analyze clinical outcomes for the infected patients (e.g., survival rate, case-fatality ratio, duration of infection, etc.)?
  4. Are you capable of doing conduct contact tracing and monitoring of close contacts?
  5. Have you assessed the current level of diagnostic testing in your community and who is providing the service, by determining:
    1. If sufficient test kits are available?
    2. If health providers are able to test all whom they think need diagnosis?
    3. If it is possible for those with symptoms to self-identify and safely access diagnostic tests without exposing others?
    4. If testing is inadequate, is there an existing plan or timeline for expanding availability?
    5. For individually detected cases, are they traceable to known sources?
  6. Are you continuing to pursue a strategy of containment through contact tracing and monitoring of close contacts?

    7. PHASE

    Individually detected cases,
    traceable to know sources of transmission

  7. Is there a lag time for receiving the laboratory and clinical data and, if so, what is that amount of time? What actions can be taken to reduce lag time?
  8. Has a threshold been identified past which you will reduce contact tracing efforts and and redirect those resources to protect those in high-risk setting?
  9. Will you assess the effectiveness of inbound traveler screening Taking into account the resources required?
  10. If cases have been detected in your community, what is the best estimate of the degree of existing transmission:
    1. For initial community transmissions, are they confirmed or are there suspected individual cases unlinked to known sources?
    2. For large-scale community transmission?

KEY ACTION #3

3

Delay or reduce transmission, #FlattenTheCurve

Slowing and limiting transmission through a community is a critical way of reducing the human cost of the outbreak, and ensuring that hospitals will be able to deliver lifesaving and life sustaining care. It can also be highly disruptive, as social distancing measures must become more aggressive in proportion to the exponential spread of the virus. Determinations on the best means of limiting transmission will depend on local factors, but in general should be calibrated to be more aggressive than what observable local conditions might intuitively suggest.

    PHASE

    No detected cases
    No burden on healthcare

  1. Have you considered local factors, including the epidemiology of the disease, characteristics of your community, healthcare capacity, and public health capacity, when determining mitigation strategies for:
    1. Populations considered most vulnerable to illness?
    2. Populations that may be more impacted socially or economically?
    3. Ways to ensure the safety and social well-being of these groups?
  2. Are your community engagement efforts specifically targeting peripheral or marginalized populations, including undocumented populations?
  3. With respect to specific community stakeholders for school leadership (public or private):
    1. Do you have clear criteria for when to recommend cancelling schools?
    2. Do you have clear and open lines of communication with school and district leadership?
    3. Have your schools established mitigation and detection strategies?
    4. Have you developed and tested remote teaching and learning methods in case they are needed?
    5. If schools are cancelled, are there mechanisms in place to care for children who need additional resources, and to incentivize all children to stay at home?
    6. Do you have a plan for providing low-income families with meals?
    7. Have the major corporations in your city issued statements and implemented best practices that seek to limit spread and serve as an example for smaller businesses?
    8. Is there adequate financial support for businesses in your community to provide paid sick leave to discourage disease spread?
    9. Do you have plans in place to ensure essential services continue despite large number of absences?
    10. Do you have plans in place to ensure that other critical infrastructure continues to operate?
  4. Do you have a plan to incentivize interventions that can slow community spread of disease?
  5. Are there penalties for non-compliance (e.g., fines, criminal charges, etc.) with interventions? If so, are you coordinating with the judicial system?
  6. Do you have the legal authority to implement interventions that can slow community spread of disease in your community - social distancing, quarantine, isolation measures?

    7. PHASE

    Individually detected cases,
    traceable to know sources of transmission

  7. Do you know the general number of cases in your community?
  8. Have you considered the public safety sector (police, military, or civil defense) to assist, if necessary, in the enforcement of social distancing?
  9. For all upcoming major conferences, sporting events, concerts, rallies, or other events where large numbers of people may gather, does your EOC have:
    1. A list of events with contact information for organizers?
    2. Have your health officials conducted a risk assessment for these events?
    3. Are you in contact with event organizers ahead of time?

    PHASE

    Individually detected cases,
    traceable to know sources of transmission

  10. Has your community established guidance criteria or official limitations on gatherings consistent with current public health guidance (as of this date it is 50 people or above)?
  11. Are you engaging with key community, civil society, business, and religious leaders to ensure their active cooperation on social distancing measures?
  12. Are support options available for populations who may face obstacles to complying with social distancing guidance?
  13. Have you engaged school officials and other relevant stakeholders to consider closing schools as well as developing the conditions for reopening them?
  14. Have you worked with local businesses to put into place measures that might reduce community transmission such as implementing telework, reducing hours of operation, and limiting travel?
  15. Do you regularly re-evaluate your course of action and adapt your policies based on local factors?
  16. Do you have the support of political leaders and the public?
  17. Have you closed schools with clear guidance on conditions for re-opening them?
  18. Have you called for the closure of non-essential businesses?
  19. For areas of intense community transmission, have you considered mandatory quarantine? Have you worked with the appropriate authorities to enforce quarantine measures?

    20. PHASE

    Individually detected cases,
    traceable to know sources of transmission

  20. Is there a lag time for receiving the laboratory and clinical data and, if so, what is that amount of time? What actions can be taken to reduce lag time?
  21. Has a threshold been identified past which you will reduce contact tracing efforts and and redirect those resources to protect those in high-risk setting?
  22. Will you assess the effectiveness of inbound traveler screening Taking into account the resources required?
  23. If cases have been detected in your community, what is the best estimate of the degree of existing transmission:
    1. For initial community transmissions, are they confirmed or are there suspected individual cases unlinked to known sources?
    2. For large-scale community transmission?
